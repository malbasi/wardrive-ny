Looking for Mass Cellular Surveillance Devices

By Matthew Albasi

An Invisible Ocean

New York City has an abundance of signals. The constant chatter of other people, the hum of traffic, the visual information from the tower buildings. In NY, The senses are constantly bombarded with information from all directions. But even if we closed our eyes and somehow quieted the people and stopped the cars, the city is still inundated with signals. While imperceptible to us, the invisible RF signals bounced from rooftop to rooftop connect each of the millions of cellphones and devices which connect us to one another.

These invisible signals are easy to forget. Most consumer gadgets work to hide the complicated underpinnings of how they work. A cellphone would never ask which cell tower it should connect to or which satellite the GPS should use. Instead, a sleek user interface covers most of the ingenious engineering to create a frictionless experience. Most of the time, hiding these complicated tidbits make the user’s experience all the better, but occasionally it creates security flaws that governments, law enforcement and criminals can use to their advantage. That’s the case with your cellphone, some of the most basic, fundamental engineering protocols that underlie every call and text are broken and we have no idea who is taking advantage of this or how often it is broken.

Wardrive

Before setting out to find Stingrays in NY, we had to devise a strategy. We knew the types of places that might be vulnerable to attack and the people who might have access to the equipment, so we began to map the points of interest across Manhattan. We were limited by the number of devices and the time available, so we constructed a few routes that would cover most of the areas we were interested in.

To find a stingray we would need to be in the right place at the right time. In other words, one of our sensors would need to be within range of a Stingray (around 0.5 km) at the exact time it was powered on. We needed an overlap of time and place. After a few practice runs to test the devices and the readouts, and one incredibly long day on a city bike, we had a basic lay of the land. Now it was time to start serious collection.

Over the course of four days and 105 miles, the sensor was driven around the city on the back of a bike messenger, Sandro. After responding to a Craigslist ad and with the promise of $50 a day, Sandro set out to work while passively collecting information about every cell tower he passed. Hopefully, if the fates were in our favor, he would be in the right place at the right time and capture a stingray.







If these devices were in use everywhere, there is a good chance Sandro would have passed one. The methods used for this analysis are not bulletproof. Foremost among them is the ‘right time right place’ requirement of the devices. It could be that stingrays were in use around the city during our sweeps and the device simply wasn’t nearby while they were on.

Another thing is newer versions of IMSI catchers could have avoided our detection. Our devices were searching on the 3g network that was in popular use until 4g became the dominant network in the US around 2009. Some IMSI catchers force the target phone to use the 3g network because it has more security flaws that can be exploited, but newer IMSI catchers are thought to work completely on 4g.
